1. Limits and Continuity ▼

Understanding the Boundary

Limits describe the behavior of a function as it gets arbitrarily close to a specific point, but not necessarily reaching it. It is the core concept that builds the entirety of calculus.

\( \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \)

Continuity requires three conditions at a point \( x = c \):

  1. \( f(c) \) is defined.
  2. The limit \( \lim_{x \to c} f(x) \) exists.
  3. \( \lim_{x \to c} f(x) = f(c) \)
2. Derivatives ▼

Rates of Change

The derivative measures the sensitivity to change of the function value with respect to a change in its argument. Graphically, it represents the slope of the tangent line to the curve at any point.

\( f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} \)

Common Rules:

  • Power Rule: \( \frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = nx^{n-1} \)
  • Product Rule: \( (fg)' = f'g + fg' \)
  • Chain Rule: \( \frac{d}{dx} [f(g(x))] = f'(g(x))g'(x) \)
3. Applications of Derivatives ▼

Real-World Optimization

Derivatives are used to find maximums, minimums, and rates of growth. Applications include related rates (e.g., how fast the water level rises in a cone) and optimization problems (e.g., maximizing the volume of a box).

If \( f'(x) = 0 \), the point \( x \) is a critical point where local extremums may lie.

4. Introduction to Integrals ▼

Accumulation and The Area Under a Curve

Integration is the reverse process of differentiation. The definite integral computes the signed area of the region in the xy-plane bounded by the graph of the function.

\( \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a) \)

This is known as the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, directly linking integration and differentiation.
